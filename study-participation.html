STUDY PARTICIPATION

Can I join the
GOAL study?

Your parent or guardian must agree for you to join the study and you will be asked to agree too.

  • You may be able to take part in the GOAL study if you:


  • ›

    are 12 to 17 years of age and have been diagnosed with urinary incontinence (UI) due to Overactive Bladder (OAB)

  • ›

    have been treated before with one or more medicines called anticholinergics (such as oxybutynin or tolterodine), but this treatment didn’t help your UI, or you don’t want to take it any more

What will I need to do?

If you continue in the study for 2 years of the study, you would visit the study center approximately 12 times and have up to 7 telephone calls with your study doctor or nurse. There are 3 main stages to the GOAL study:

Stage 1

Screening Visit (your first visit). The doctor will ask you some questions and do some tests to see if you are okay and willing to join the study.

Stage 2

Treatment Visit (when you have the study treatment). You will be given one of three different strengths (doses) of medicine that will be injected inside your bladder. You and your doctor will not be able to pick, or know, which strength you have. Your doctor will try to make sure these injections do not hurt by giving you pain relief. You will be given the study treatment at least once. You may be given more study medicine if you ask and your doctor agrees that you need it.

Stage 3

Follow-up Visits (about 2 weeks after you have had your study treatment and regularly after that) for your doctor to see how you are doing.

What test will I have?

Important: patients who are pregnant must not take part in this study. If you are a girl who has started having periods (menstruating), you will need to take pregnancy tests just so the doctors can be sure you are not pregnant.

  • All the way through the GOAL study, your doctor will need to do different tests to collect information about how well you feel and how well the treatment is working.


  • The study doctor or nurse will do things like:

  • ›

    measure how tall you are and how much you weigh

  • ›

    test your blood and urine

  • ›

    check your pulse, temperature, breathing rate and blood pressure

  • ›

    a painless ultrasound scan to take pictures of your kidneys and bladder


  • The study doctor or nurse will ask you (or your parent or guardian):

  • ›

    questions about your health

  • ›

    to keep a bladder diary for 2 days (usually at the weekend) before seeing the doctor. You may need to drink more than usual during your diary days.

Will the study help me?

  • Being in the GOAL study might not help you. Your condition could get better, stay the same or get worse. Doctors have to follow strict rules to make sure they keep the patients that take part in the study safe and well looked after. By taking part in this study you could be helping doctors learn more about how to help others with this condition in the future.
