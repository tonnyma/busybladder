THE WHAT

What is Overactive Bladder and how common is it in children?

In children, Overactive Bladder(OAB) is a condition where the child may experience one or more of the following:

a sudden need to urinate(pee) right away, which is called “urgency”


urinating more than normal during the day, which is called “frequency”


leaking urine by accident, which is called “incontinence”


waking up at night to urinate, which is called “nocturia”



In children ages 3-17, OAB is more common in girls than boys, and usually goes away as the child gets older However, it can be frustrating, embarrassing and can affect quality of life by making it difficult to do normal everyday things.

It is difficult to say how common OAB is in children but it is the most common voiding (or bladder emptying) dysfunction. As a parent, it is important to notice what children do to avoid experiencing these events. Children may drink less fluids and hold themselves in positions to avoid having to go to the bathroom. They may have more incontinence episodes later in the day because they are tired and cannot concentrate as well.


So, how does a normal bladder work and what causes Overactive Bladder?

The Normal Bladder:

Here is a cartoon that shows how it works. The bladder is like a balloon that can hold about 1-2 cups of urine. Urine is made from the kidneys. The bladder muscles are relaxed as it fills with urine. Once filled, a signal is sent to the brain letting the child know it’s time to go to the bathroom. The bladder will contract (squeeze) when going to the bathroom.



The Cause of OAB in Children

What causes OAB in children is not completely understood. It is thought that overactive bladder happens in children because signals from the brain to the bladder that control the muscles causing the bladder to contract and release urine/pee, are not fully developed.

What is it like to live with overactive bladder?

Living with OAB and how it affects qualify of life can be different for each child. Having an accident can be upsetting, embarrassing, disruptive and make children feel bad. Having to go to the bathroom a lot may prevent children from participating in fun activities like sports and sleepovers. Some children may try to overcome the frequent bathroom trips by “holding it in” which can cause stomach aches and urinary tract infections. Many children will not speak to their parents about this but should. Seeing a doctor is important and can help children lead full and active lives.

What treatments are available for overactive bladder?

There are several treatments available to help children with their overactive bladder and you may have tried them already.
Anticholinergics reduce the strength of the brain’s signals to squeeze the bladder and cause symptoms of OAB to be reduced. These treatments can be effective in some children, but in others may not be completely effective or may have side-effects (such as dry mouth, constipation and blurred vision) which can become bothersome over time.
There are other treatments that can be given by a doctor, which could also be effective, but again may not be as effective in some children and could have unwanted side effects
Electro-stimulation/neuromodulators may be effective but are put under the skin, can cause infections, and can have technical issues
If the above treatments don’t work, in some cases bladder surgery may be suggested
Speaking about these potential treatments with a doctor is important

Finding Better Solutions

Until now, if these treatments were ineffective or bothersome, the only alternative was to just live with the condition. However, a new medicine is being investigated which could help better control overactive bladder.
